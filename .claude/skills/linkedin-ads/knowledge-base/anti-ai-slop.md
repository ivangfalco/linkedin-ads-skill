# Anti-AI-Slop - Hard Rules for Copy

Every line of ad copy clears this audit before it ships. If it fails, rewrite it.

## Why this matters
Models trained on years of LinkedIn posts default to the most-imitated patterns. Readers have seen those same patterns thousands of times and now pattern-match them as "AI-written" - and once that flag trips, they discount even specific, true claims. AI slop is worse than generic copy: generic copy is ignored, slop is actively distrusted.

## The 15 patterns to remove

1. **Setup/reveal** - "Most people think X. They're actually Y." → State the real claim directly, with a specific or a source.
2. **Rule of three** - "The same X. The same Y. The same Z." → One concrete example beats three parallel fragments.
3. **Contrast pivot** - "Different X. Different Y. Same Z." → Name the actual universal in one sentence with a real case.
4. **Negation reveal** - "That's not X. It's Y." → State Y with its evidence.
5. **Compound "not just"** - "It's not just a CRM, it's a platform." → Say what it does in plain functional terms.
6. **Em-dash overload** - dashes inserted for pacing. → Use periods or commas. Short dash only, never em dash.
7. **Fragment stacking** - single words on their own lines for drama ("Focus. Clarity. Results."). → Write the sentence.
8. **"Here's the thing" / "Let me be clear" openers** → Cut the filler, make the assertion.
9. **Triple-adjective stacking** - "dynamic, ever-evolving, fast-paced." → One precise word, or none.
10. **"In today's [hyperbolic] world" openers** → Open with the specific observation that made it worth writing.
11. **Mystery hooks** - "There's something most marketers miss..." → Just say the observation.
12. **Engagement-question closers** - "What's your take?" → End on the strongest point or a real CTA.
13. **"This is why X matters" closers** → Trust the reader; end on the implication.
14. **Fragment chains** - short sentences strung together for a beat where one flowing sentence is natural. → Write the full sentence.
15. **Parallel-anchor pacing** - "AI budgets grew. Revenue didn't." (two parallel sentences, second ending on a short contrastive anchor). → One declarative or compound sentence.

## Reference voice
Write like the body copy on serious enterprise SaaS sites (Linear, Stripe, Databricks): full declarative sentences with real subjects and verbs, specific named things, plain English over performance, active voice. Authoritative because of what's said, not how it's performed.

- Not "Built for scale." → "Handles hundreds of interdependent KPIs at enterprise scale."
- Not "industry-leading accuracy." → name the number and the benchmark.

## The audit (do this on every ad)
Read it aloud.
- Does it sound like a real person in this industry talking? If it sounds like a LinkedIn post, it's slop.
- More than one dash per paragraph? Cut.
- Any of the 15 patterns present? Rewrite.
- Every claim point to a real source or number? If not, it was invented to fill space - drop it or source it.

An ad can go through 2-3 audit passes. That's normal. If it keeps failing, the underlying idea is weak and the copy is leaning on structure to compensate - go back to the insight.
