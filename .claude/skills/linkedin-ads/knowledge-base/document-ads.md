# Document Ads - Best Practices

LinkedIn document ads (carousel format) let users swipe through multiple slides directly in the feed. Strong for MOF nurture - they educate, build trust, and retarget well.

## Format & Specs

- **Slide size:** 1080 x 1350px (portrait 4:5) - ALWAYS. Never 1:1 square for document ads. Square (1080x1080) is for single image ads only.
- **Slide count:** 5-7 for MOF. More than 7 risks drop-off. Fewer than 5 feels thin.
- **File format:** PDF upload (each page = one slide)
- **Can be gated** (Lead Gen Form) or ungated
- **Post body:** Visible above the carousel in the feed. Keep concise - tease the content, don't summarize it.

## 7-Slide Campaign Formula

Tested structure for MOF document ads:

| Slide | Role | Content |
|-------|------|---------|
| 1 | Hook | 3 hook options for A/B testing. Strong headline + subtitle. |
| 2-3 | Pain / cost | What the audience is dealing with. Different per audience segment. |
| 4 | Principle / shift | The "aha" moment. A reframe, a key stat, or a principle that changes how they see the problem. |
| 5-6 | Product solution | What the solution looks like + what the product specifically does. Shared across audiences but framing differs. |
| 7 | CTA | Demo booking. Clean, minimal, brand messaging. |

### Key rule: Slides 5 and 6 must NOT be redundant.

This is the most common structural mistake. Slide 5 describes what any capable system needs (generic requirements). Slide 6 describes what the specific product delivers (unique differentiators). If both slides list the same capabilities with different words, merge or differentiate.

**Good differentiation example:**
- Slide 5: "Multilingual content management - all language versions in one place" (what any solution needs)
- Slide 6: "24 languages with automatic redirection based on user location" (what THIS product uniquely does)

**Bad (redundant):**
- Slide 5: "Multilingual content management"
- Slide 6: "Language management across 24+ markets"

## Copy Principles

### Each slide = one distinct purpose
If you can't state the purpose in 5 words, the slide is unfocused. Write the purpose before writing the copy.

### Use the brand's actual bridge phrases
The transition from education to product is critical. Never introduce the product cold. Every brand has a characteristic way of bridging - check the brand's tone profile for their specific pattern. Without a bridge, the product slide feels like a pitch instead of a logical conclusion.

### CTA slides: declarative, not interrogative
Don't use questions in CTA slides ("If you've ever dealt with...") unless the brand explicitly uses that pattern. Use:
- A clear statement of what the product does
- Recurring brand messaging points the brand already uses
- A specific, gentle CTA matching the brand's CTA style

### Statistics deserve their own slide
A powerful stat buried in a paragraph is wasted. Give it a standalone visual break slide:
- Big number in accent color, large type (120-150pt recommended)
- Minimal surrounding text
- Source attribution in small gray text
- This breaks up text-heavy slides and creates visual rhythm

### Post body conventions
- Tease the content, don't summarize all 7 slides
- Match the brand's formatting conventions (check brand profile for specific symbols/formatting they use)
- Match the tone of the slides - the post body and carousel should feel like one piece

## Design Principles

### Every slide needs a design note
Don't leave visual treatment to chance. Specify:
- Text hierarchy (what's bold, what size, what color)
- Content block fills (use the brand palette for background fills on bullet sections)
- Visual elements (diagrams, screenshots, mockups)
- Whitespace expectations

### Text density kills engagement
Document ads are swiped quickly. If a slide looks like a wall of text, people skip it.
- **Max:** One lead-in sentence + one bullet list per slide, OR one short paragraph + one visual element
- **Never:** Two paragraphs + bullet list on one slide
- **Use content blocks** (light-colored fills from the brand palette) to visually separate bullet lists from surrounding text

### Product slides need visual options
Always provide:
- **Option A:** Real product UI screenshot (if available)
- **Option B:** Designed mockup (browser frame, simplified interface, placeholder data)

This gives flexibility for when the real screenshot isn't available yet.

### Flow diagrams > text descriptions
When showing processes or chains, use a visual diagram with connected nodes. Don't describe a process flow in a paragraph - show it.

## Common Mistakes

| Mistake | Why it's bad | Fix |
|---------|-------------|-----|
| Adjacent slides that say the same thing | Feels repetitive, wastes a slide | Merge or differentiate (generic requirements vs. specific differentiators) |
| Product introduced without bridge phrase | Feels like a pitch, not a conclusion | Use the brand's characteristic transition phrase |
| Questions in body/CTA when brand doesn't do that | Breaks brand consistency, feels like marketing | Use declarative statements + recurring messaging |
| Unverified claims (especially technical/regulatory) | Destroys credibility with expert audiences | Every claim must trace to verified source material |
| Text-heavy slides with no visual treatment | People swipe past without reading | Content blocks, visual breaks, design notes |
| Dramatic/informal language for measured brands | Audience detects and distrusts it | Use the brand's actual vocabulary |
| Burying key stats in paragraphs | Wastes the most powerful proof point | Standalone visual break slide |
| Same product slide for different audiences | Feels generic, misses audience-specific concerns | Same product, different framing per audience |

## Audience Variant Strategy

When creating the same document ad for multiple audience segments:

| Element | Shared | Different |
|---------|--------|-----------|
| Slide 1 (Hook) | Format, design | Headline, subtitle, pain angle |
| Slides 2-3 (Pain) | - | Fully different - each audience has different daily frustrations |
| Slide 4 (Principle) | Can share the concept | Framing differs per audience's primary concern |
| Slides 5-6 (Product) | Product capabilities are the same | Framing emphasizes what matters most to each audience |
| Slide 7 (CTA) | CTA action (book demo) | Opening statement tailored to audience role |
| Post body | General structure | Pain points and framing tailored |
